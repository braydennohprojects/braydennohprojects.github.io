Boulder Transport

The ratio of boulder counts between two reaches is set entirely by the flood statistics and bed slopes — the transport law cancels.

nup(D) / ndn(D) = φdn(D) / φup(D)

Bed slope: 10% → 1%.
▮ Upstream: 10%–9%
▮ Downstream: 2%–1%

1. When does a boulder move?

A boulder of diameter D is entrained when the flow depth exceeds the Shields threshold, hc = Θc(ρs−ρ)D / (ρ S), which increases linearly with grain size and inversely with bed slope. A hiding function (hc ∝ Dβ, adjustable via the β slider) can weaken this size dependence, but for grains much larger than the bed median the effect is small.

2. Why individual boulders don't work

We don't know when each boulder entered the channel or how many floods moved it. A boulder far downstream could have arrived millennia ago or last year. Individual transport histories can't be reconstructed.

3. The ratio test

Instead, count boulders of each size in two reaches and take the ratio. Boulders accumulate where they're hard to move, so:

nup(D) / ndn(D) = φdn(D) / φup(D)  φ = fraction of floods exceeding the threshold

What cancels: (1) hop distance (same D at both reaches), (2) flood rate (same hydrograph), (3) source production (same flux). Only the bed slopes and flood distribution matter.

5. Dating the regime change

After a regime shift, small boulders equilibrate fast (they move often) but large boulders lag (they rarely move). The crossover diameter D* where dots start deviating from the curve tells you when the change happened:

a. Find D* from the ratio plot (where dots leave the curve).
b. Compute φ(D*) from the gauge record.
c. Time since change: tc ≈ 1 / (λ · φ(D*)).
d. Example: D* = 0.8 m, φ = 10−3 → τ ≈ 1000 floods (centuries to millennia).

This gives the right order of magnitude, not a precise year. But it easily separates “last century” from “early Holocene.” Limitation: the method only detects transitions toward calmer conditions. The reverse (calm → extreme) erases itself — the new extreme floods flush the old signal.
